Alright, now that we have the foundation, lets jump into analyzing these networks! I'll start with some simple characterization of the degree distribution (that is, the collection of all pairwise interactions) for each network. Then, I will move into some more complicated network structures that take the whole network and its patterns of interconnectedness into account. Finally, I will show you a little motif or subgraph analysis, in which we look at the frequencies of different patterns of connection between all possible subsets of three nodes.

Perhaps the simplest measure of network structure is the distribution of interaction strengths, termed the degree distribution. These histograms include all of the pairwise interaction strengths, that is, the degree of niche overlap between each pair of isolates. One step up in complexity of network structure is to consider the relationship between incoming and outgoing links in the same node. Here we plot the in-degree against the outdegree for each node in our netwokrks, dividing nodes according to the node's kingdom and the treatment of the host plant.

Note that all of these lines have negative slopes — nodes with high indegrees tend to have low outdegree and vice versa. Put another way, there are some isolates which we think of as strong competitors: they overlap many other isolates' niches, but do not often have their own niche significantly overlapped; and there are some isolates we could think of as weak competitors: overlapping few others, yet having many overlap their own niche.

While this framework of measuring the degree of niche overlap between each pair of isolates provides a high level of detail about the structure of iteractions across the community, many of the most popular metrics of network structure are defined only in the case of binary networks, that is networks where the links only indicate presence or absence of an interaction, and do not provide information on the strength of any given interaction.

To convert our weighted interactions into binary ones, we need only define a threshold

Comparing isolate kingdoms

Metrics of local network structure are calcuated for each node of the network, so we can use then to compare bacterial and fungal isolates within each network.

One of the most commonly utilized network structures is that of centrality. There are many different formulations for centrality, but they all boil down to assigning importance to each node in the network. Where they differ is in how they define "importance". Here, we report two measures of centrality that have some biological relevance to the system we are working with: degree and closeness.

An isolate with high degree centrality has the potential to directly influence many of its neighbors, but an isolate with high closeness centrality has greater potential to spread its influence throughout the network as a whole.

We also report differences in the clustering coefficient of each node. This value is calculated by counting how many of an isolate's partners interact with each other, forming triangular patterns in the interaction network.

Each box in this table is a network, colored according to the sign and significance of differences between isolates of each of the two kingdoms: blue indicates that bacteria have larger values for this metric, red indicates fungal isolates do. Increasing darkness signifies smaller p-values: < 0.05 (light), < 0.01 (medium), and < 0.001 (dark). As can be seen, bacteria and fungi differ often in the control plants, but very rarely in the nutrient supplemented ones. This suggests some sort of equalizing force being applied in the NPK treatment.

Comparing network treatments

For network-wide structures, we calculate a single value for each network, precluding inter-kingdom comparisons, so here we are instead comparing metrics across treatments. Because of this, we only have two comparisons for each metric: the binary and the weighted case. As before, we color each box in favor of the larger value, with increasing significance being depicted by increasing color intensity.

When we compare the 6 values for each of the control networks to the 6 values for NPK in each of these metrics (using t-tests), we see very few differences in structure between treatments. That is, control and NPK plants tend to have similarly structured networks, despite the observed differences in degree distribution.

(Note that these results are qualitatively independent from the inclusion/exclusion of the outlying control network)

Comparing network treatments — against random

One of the issues with comparing networks directly is that there can be confounding differences for instance in size and connectance that are not as biologically relevant. to get around, do randomizations
explain table
many "significant" differences, but they are quite small

Whle comparing two sets of networks directly has the potential to provide insights about differences between treatments, there is also a risk of detecting differences that are less biologically relevant due simply to differences in size or connectance between the groups.

This can be overcome by comparing networks against randomizations of their own structure, where the randomization protocol perserves some aspects of the structure, but not others. Here, we use a configuration model randomization, which preserves the degree distribution, but allows larger scale structures to vary more freely.

Again, we indicate each network by one icon in each row. There are twenty-four icons because we count each network twice: once to depict its p-value (that is, what proportion of random networks are less than or greater than the empirically observed value for that web), and again to depict the z-score (that is, how much larger or smaller than the mean of the randomizations is the empirically observed value). Empirical values larger than the expectation are depicted with a or , for p-values > 0.95 or > 0.99, respectively and z-scores > 2 or > 4, respectively. Analogously, empirical values smaller than the expectation are depicted using or . Hyphens indicate nonsignificantly different empirical values and x's indicate cases where there were insufficient unique randomizations to calculate p-values or z-scores (here we use a cutoff of 20 unique randomizations).

Note that, while there are many significant differences in terms of p-values, they are almost all very small in terms of z-score.

Alright, that was kindof a whilrlwind of results, but what does it all mean? Over the final few slides, I will breifly sythesize some of these results and try to tie it all back to the biological implications.

Comparing Kingdom

  • Degree distributions differ significantly between interaction types
    • in particular, fungi tend to have a larger impact on bacteria than vice versa (especially for control plants)
  • Isolates cluster by kingdom
    • especially in weighted networks (though more noticeable in binary!)
  • Bacteria and fungi differ in their node-specific structure only in control plants

Comparing Treatment

  • Degree distributions also differ significantly beween treatments

But...

  • There are few / weak differences in network-wide structure between treatments
  • (Binary) networks show many significant deviations from their random expectations
    • but these differences tended to be small

Conclusions

  • Nutrient ammendments level the playing field
    • Fungi tend to have wider niches and greater overlap on bacterial isolates
    • But these differences are attenuated in NPK plants
  • Despite pairwise differences, differences not visible in larger network structure
  • These small resource competition networks seem to be dominated by pairwise effects
    • No evidence of higher-order interactions
