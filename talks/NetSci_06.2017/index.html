We see a surprising number of species coexisting in nature. One of the places this is most apparent is in tropical rainforests. This diversity is expecially noteworthy when one considers the onslaught of perturbing forces these communities endure. As a theoretical ecologist, we would like to develop a theory that allows for the stable coexistence of many species while maintaining a robustness to parameters. The other striking thing about this picture is how packed the trees are. Every square meter is taken up and all of the trees are about the same size --- ZERO SUM DYNAMICS TALK MORE ABOUT ECOLOGY!! In many ways, solving this problem comes down to figuring out what happens when an individual dies or, put another way, how the replacement is chosen to fill the gap.[NEXT SLIDE]

Model Assumptions

$$\sum_i x_i = 1$$

For the rest of the talk, we will be assuming zero-sum dynamics and will thus be modelling the relative species abundances $x_i$. More concretely, this means that [NEXT SLIDE] when a tree dies [NEXT SLIDE] a seedling is chosen at random to fill the gap [NEXT SLIDE] this seedling grows into an adult [NEXT SLIDE] maintaining the total number of individuals in the community but potentially changing the relative abundances of the species ivolved.[NEXT SLIDE]

Building a Model

$$\propto f_i x_i$$

$$\begin{align}\mathcal{P}(i \text{ wins against } j) &= H_{ij}\\ &= 1 - H_{ji}\end{align}$$

$$d_i x_i$$

Each species, thus has a [NEXT SLIDE] birth rate, which is proportional to its fertility (the number of seedlings produced). [NEXT SLIDE] A death rate. We assume that a random individual dies at each time point. Since the individual is chosen at random, the probability of any individual dying is proportional to its relative species abundance. And, finally what happens in-between these events [NEXT SLIDE]: some kind of competition. We can assume very simplified competition, where the individuals compete in pairs and the winner of any competitive bout is determined by a tournament matrix $\textbf{H}$ which encapsulates the probabilities of any species $i$ beating another species $j$. Of course, this means species $j$ has a $1-H{ij}=H_{ji}$ probability of winning the bout.[NEXT SLIDE]

Using this simple framework, we can evision some models that have already been well studied, as well as some potential advancements. For instance, the neutral theory of biodiversity proposed by Stephen Hubbell in 2001 can be replicated in this framework by assuming [NEXT SLIDE] that the fecundities and death rates are equal for all species and that the probability of any given species winning a competitive bout is equal to that of its competitor winning. Likewise, if the probabilities of victory are exclusively $1$'s and $0$'s, that is, species $i$ always wins against species $j$ and always loses to some species $k$, we are dealing with competitive tournaments, which have been explored in an ecological context by Allesina and Levine in PNAS in 2011. In this work, we build on these models [NEXT SLIDE] by first allowing non-binary probabilities of victory and second [NEXT SLIDE] allowing full flexibility of parameterization. [NEXT SLIDE] We will first consider the former. [NEXT SLIDE]

Building a Model: Equilvalence with Replicator Equation

$$\begin{align} \dfrac{\text{d} x_i }{\text{d} t} &= x^2_i + 2 x_i \sum_{j \neq i} H_{ij} x_j - x_i\\ &= x_i \left(2 \sum_j H_{ij} x_j - 1 \right)\\ &= \sum_j \left( H_{ij} - H_{ji}\right) x_i x_j\\ &= \sum_j P_{ij} x_i x_j \end{align}$$

One of the really nice things about this model formulation is that it can be shown to be equivalent to the extremely well studied replicator equations, using the notation from game theory of a payoff matrix $\mathbf{P}$ which dictates how much each species gains (or loses) as a result of each competitive bout.[NEXT SLIDE]

Building a Model: Example

$$\mathbf{H} = \left[\begin{array} 00.5 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.5 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0.5 \end{array}\right] $$


$$\mathbf{P} = \left[\begin{array} 0~~~0 & ~~~1 & -1 \\ -1 & ~~~0 & ~~~1 \\ ~~~1 & -1 & ~~~0 \end{array}\right]$$

Lets look at an example, for instance, imagine three species: rock, paper, and scissors which interact in an intransitive cycle of complete dominance. The tournament matrix $\mathbf{H}$ is thus shown here, with a $1$ in the top middle, for instance, indicating that paper always wins against rock. An individual of a given species always has an equal probability of winning or losing when competing against another individual of the same species, hence the $0.5$ on the diagonal. This $\mathbf{H}$ corresponds to the following $\mathbf{P}$, which shows that, in the previously mentioned bout, the result is that rock loses an individual and paper gains one. These dynamics result is neutral cycling about an equilibrium of one third for each species.[NEXT SLIDE]

Coexistence

How many species do we expect to survive?

Method

  1. Generate many random (hyper) tournaments of $s$ species by drawing interaction strengths($H_{ij}$) at random
  2. Run dynamics
  3. Count the $k$ survivors
  4. what is $\mathcal{P}(k|s)$?


$$\mathcal{P}(k|s) = \begin{cases} 0 &\text{for } k \text{ even}\\ {{s}\choose{k}} 2^{1-s} &\text{otherwise}\end{cases}$$

Result

So, we saw that in some cases, all of the species will survive and in other cases, some will go extinct. It is a reasonable step to then wonder how many species will survive on average. Put another way, if we were to open all the gates at a zoo and let the animals interact, how many species would we expect to coexist after all of the extinctions? We can approach this problem from the perspective of random matrices. In this approach, we generate a random tournament matrix of probabilities and then run the dynamics to see how many of the original species survive. If we do this many times, we can then calculate the expected number. [NEXT SLIDE]. When we do this, we find that this probability is $0$ for an even number of surviving species and follows a binomial distribution otherwise. This means that, regardless of the number of initial species, the expected number that survive is half of that. Put another way, there is no upper limit for the number of surviving species, which suggests that the high biodiversity we observe in nature maybe shoudldn't be so surprising after all. [NEXT SLIDE]

Conclusions

For any matrix $\mathbf{H}$ on $s$ species

  • Irrespective of (positive) initial conditions, $k$ species will coexist and $s - k$ will go extinct
  • Those that coexist, cycle neutrally around the equilibrium point $x^\ast$
  • We can choose $x^\ast$ arbitrarily

For a random $\mathbf{H}$ on $s$ species

  • On average, $k = s/2$
So, where do we stand thus far? We have a model that can produce high biodiversity without fine tuning. It can, though I did not go into it here, yield an arbitrary species abundance distribution as equilibrium with the proper choice of $\mathbf{H}$. There are still some shortcomings, namely, these neutral cycles, which are not only biologically unrealistic, but also make their species more susceptible to extinction as perturbations, on average, increase the size of such neutral cycles. Higher-order Interactions

At this point, you might be wondering why this is being presented in a session on higher-order interactions (HOI). So far, the interaction part of our model consisted of choosing two seedlings and competing them to determine the winner in a pairwise fasion. In nature, we observe a rich seedbank, where potentially many seedlings are competing for each gap in the canopy. [NEXT SLIDE] We can take a step toward this reality by increasing the number of competing species in each bout to three.[NEXT SLIDE]

Parameterizing HOI

Unfortunately, even this small change faces us with a dilemma. We can consider true HOI in which there is a coeficient for each unique triplet of interacting species, but then our number of parameters in the model grows exponentially. An alternative is to consider an interaction chain, [NEXT SLIDE] in which first two of the species compete and then the winner competes with the remaining species to determine the overall winner. This has the advantage of reducing the number of parameters. This interaction chain dynamic could result from a separation of space or time scale between the interaction and birth/death phases. [NEXT SLIDE]

Still Consistent with Replicator Dynamics

$$\dfrac{\text{d} x_i }{\text{d} t} = \sum_j P_{ij} x_i x_j$$ $$\dfrac{\text{d} x_i }{\text{d} t} = \sum_j P_{ijk} x_i x_j x_k$$

where $\mathbf{P}$ is a tensor in which $$P_{ijk} = 2 H_{ij} H_{ik} - H_{ji} H_{jk} - H_{ki} H_{kj}$$

This change does not break equivalence with replicator dynamics. The only change is that the payoff matrix now becomes [NEXT SLIDE] a payoff tensor with as many indices as there are interacting seedlings. Because we are defining the HOI in terms of a sequence of pairwise competitive bouts, we can write this tensor $\mathbf{p}$ in terms of the tournament matrix $\mathbf{H}$. [NEXT SLIDE]

Conclusions

  • The addition of higher order interactions stabilize the dynamics but do not change the equilibrium
  • Higher orders of higher order interactions speed up convergence to the equilibrium
  • This is all despite not adding any new information/parameters into the model!
