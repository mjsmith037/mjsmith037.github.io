Migration dampens cycles:

Reduced cycle amplitude and disease prevalence in networks with longer chains

Destinations inherit dynamics:

Interconnected parts of the network should have similar dynamics

Dynamics inherit asymmetrically:

Preponderance of cyclical and chaotic dynamics

  • Simple patterns emerge in small metapopulation networks
    • When linked with migration, cyclical dynamics are dampened further down chains
    • Migration tends to lead to inheritance of dynamical regimes between populations
    • When multiple source populations are considered, more complicated dynamics (cycles and chaos) dominate the inheritance
  • While there are patterns in larger networks, they are not simple
    • Patches with no immigration tend to have higher pathogen variance than patches that have at least one source of incoming host movement
    • Most patches exhibit oscillatory dynamics
  • Further research is needed to:
    • Identify relationships between more complex network structures and disease dynamics
    • Fit to empirical data, including empirical network structures

To do this we had to answer a couple of basic questions, such as "What is a contact?" The answer to this question is obviously context specific, but with COVID-19, with its aerosolized transmission, we think about the risk of transmission increasing with the duration of people sharing the same air space.

next slide

But of course the ways in which we are close to other people is also context dependent, leading to the key insight for this project: the structure of our interactions with other people is dependent on the different ways in which we interact with different facets of our social network.

To put it more concretely, we interact with co-workers differently than we interact with people at home, or at a religious institution. The differences in these interactions, including the strength of interaction and the interacting group size, can have consequences for the spread of disease.

Disease Model

I want to take a minute to run through the underlying disease model, but since I'm sure most of you have seen these types of models explained, and perhaps explained them yourselves, multiple times, I will keep it very brief.

At each timestep in a simulation, every individual in the network has one of six states. They start out as Susceptible to disease and can become infected when they share an edge, representing a social contact, with an infectious individual.

next slide

Upon infection, they enter a latency period and are classified as Exposed, but not yet infectious. Over time, they transition to being I/Infectious, and some fraction will develop symptoms.

next slide

As more time passes, some of these will Recover,

next slide

while others will sadly Die.

next slide

The final piece of the puzzle is that those individuals that we classified as vulnerable have both longer disease courses (slower recovery),

and are more likely to die, modifying these two parameters as shown.

So, for a single simulated epidemic, we see nodes cycling through the various disease states, and, on the right, the proportion of the population in each class through time. Note that some individuals are able to escape infection, even at the end of the simulation, if they are sparsely connected to the rest of the network, or just very lucky.

Conclusions

  • Populations are highly interconnected, allowing disease to spread even to specific individuals that are taking precautions
  • Community action is needed to protect the most vulnerable
  • Demographic structure can have nuanced, contradictory effects on disease spread
