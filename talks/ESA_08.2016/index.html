introduction

“Science is knowledge arranged and classified according to truth, facts, and the general laws of nature.” — Luther Burbank

Scientists often seek to classify observable phenomena into categories based on similarity. Sometimes these categories are spurious and later revised or rejected, while other times a more in-depth analysis reveals a concrete foundation for what was originally little more than an intuition.

In the study of food-webs, ecologists have come up with categories to classify species according to the details of thier interactions with what they eat: herbivores, omnivores, carnivores, as well by trophic considerations: primary producers, top predators. In some cases, the definitions can be non-rigorous, allowing for a degree of interpretation.

What are parasites?

  1. Prey/predator body size ratio (?)
  2. Intimate, durable interactions (?)
  3. Number of hosts or prey attacked during a particular life-history stage

Lafferty 2008

Take parasites, for example:
Lafferty, in his contribution to the Encyclopedia of Ecology, explains some of the many complications that arise when trying to systematically differentiate parasites from other life-cycles we observe in nature He discounts the first option since many predators consume prey that are larger than themselves (e.g. wolves eating moose) and the second with a similar argument: lions spend weeks consuming a single antelope.

Though he settles on the number of hosts/prey attacked in a particular life-history stage as a good indicator, with parasites attacking fewer (usually only one), this is difficult to measure quickly and precisely using the sampling methods that are common when collecting food-web data.

introduction: Food-Webs

When the additional information of ecological classifications is unavailable, e.g. with a previously unobserved species, parasites and consumers look much more similar.

This project started as a question of whether or not we can identify species roles based just on this information, that is, the network structure.

Put another way, can we max-width:7.5em;do what an ecologist does when observing species extensively in the field by just looking at the binary network of interactions?

By approaching this problem in a more statistical way, we can more explicitly identify the important, distinguishing characteristics, and provide justification (or not) for the characteristics that are thought to be important.

methods: Group Model

Also known as the stochastic blockmodel

A generalized form of Modularity

Seeks to find groups of strongly connecting species

Allesina & Pascual 2009

Ecological classifications, such as herbivore or parasite, are assigned by ecologists following a detailed investigation of the species life history and, in particular, its interactions with other species. Another approach to group species is to look at the overall network structure and to group species that eat or are eaten by the same or similar prey or predators.

In 2009, Allesina & Pascual introduced the group model to ecology to make this concept statistically rigorous. The model had been used previously in the social sciences under a different name -- the stochastic blockmodel. This model works by re-arranging the rows/columns of the matrix in order to form blocks of species with similar interactions.

One drawback of this model (in general, though not evident in this case) is that the degree -- i.e. the number of consumptive interactions a species has -- can influence the grouping in undesirable ways, such as artificially grouping specialists together just because they all have few prey.

To account for this, in this project, we also used a modified version of the group model that accounts for the differences in degree across species.

Note that, despite the difference in degree we saw in the previous figure, the degree-corrected group model produced significantly more imbalanced groups, regardless of concomitant predation (paired t-test, estimated difference: .12, p < .001 and .067, p < .001, when including/excluding concomitant predation, respectively). This suggests that degree is not an essential distinguishing factor for parasites.

Including concomitant links also produced more imbalanced groups, under both the degree-corrected (paired t-test, estimated difference: .034, p = .0049) and uncorrected (estimated difference: 203 .085, p < .001) models.

results: Condensed Graphs

A more concise way to view these networks is to collapse the groups into a single node, colored according to the ecological classifications of the nodes within it.

In this case, for example, the species have been divided into two groups, with one of the groups consisting entirely of consumers. This suggests that consumers are behaving in a unique way, i.e. the network structure of a given consumer is more similar to other consumers than to non-consumers.

When we have five groups, something intesting happens: the consumer block is split in two. This is suggestive of additional structure within these gross ecological categories. Put another way, there are sub-structures within the consumer class where the differences between the sub-categorizations are more explanatory than the difference between consumers at large and other ecological categories.

Finally, the highest number of groups we looked at: 10 groups we get some uniform groups, and some groups that are still conglomerates. This shows that while our statistical approach does resemble the ecological categorizations, there are larger statistical differences between members of the same categorizations than between different ones. Some of the cateogories are better parsed than others, which suggests that these categories have a stronger foundation in network structure.

conclusions

  1. Ecological classifications have statistical meaning
    • Especially for Consumers and Parasites
    • (beyond simple degree distribution)
  2. But this is not always the strongest source of variance
    • Further structure to be found
  3. Concomitant links important
  4. Next step: Identify the most explanatory properties of homogenous groups

So what can we take away from this?

Well, it is comforting to know that the classifications that ecologists use in the field have statistical meaning with regards to network structure, and that these differences extend beyond simple degree distribution (the degree-corrected model produced more imbalanced partitions, suggesting that discounting the effect of degree in fact improved the ability to distinguish ecological categorizations).

Interestingly, the intra-category differences were stronger than the inter-category differences in some cases, suggesting there might be more informative classifications than the one we used here.

Concomitant links are useful, but not the end-all in distinguishing parasites and consumers.

Finally, we can use these groupings (especially the homogenous ones) to identify the properties that differ between categorizations. These differences can then be used to produce more rigorous definitions and provide insight into the important distinctions between species roles in nature.
