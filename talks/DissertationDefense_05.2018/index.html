I'm going to provide a brief contextualization of network research in Ecology. Touching on some of the important concepts that will pervade the rest of the talk.

Networks as a unifying methodology

Here we are reminded that in nature
nothing exists alone
—Rachel Carson, Silent Spring

Few concepts have driven and shaped ecological research through the ages as much as the notion that everything is connected. Immortalized through famous quotes such as Darwin's (en)tangled bank, this concept can be operationalized through the tools and techniques of network analysis in which, for example, species are nodes in a network with links representing interactions between these species.

Networks as a unifying methodology

Of course, it is not just nature that is interconnected, but there are a variety of ways in which we are connected both with each other and with different parts of the global ecosystem. Indeed, "network science" has proven to be an exceedingly interdisciplinary endeavor, with similar techniques and concepts being utilized across a host of fields spanning both the physical, biological, and social sciences.

Ecological Networks

Ings & Hawes 2018

Within ecology, the use of networks is as old as the field itself. With the first food chains being documented around the same time as graph theory is being developed in the 18th century, and complex food-webs being depicted in scientific publications by the 19th century when the term "Ecology" is first coined.

Things moved quickly from there and the number of publications looking at ecological networks continues to grow exponentially to the present day.

Especially noteworthy is the more recent shift in focus to look at networks of various (and multiple) interaction types.

Network structure

  • Size (number of nodes)
  • Connectance (proportion of possible links realized)
  • Degree distribution (how many connections does each node have?)

The area of network science that most fascinates me is network structure. As networks became more commonplace, a natural question arose of how to compare networks to one another. Initially, scientists would just look at differences in the core components of networks, starting with size, that is, the number of things in the network. Represented here by these circles,

next slide

The second core component of a network is connections between the nodes, usually termed "links" or "edges." Here, these are depicted with lines connecting the dots. notice that nodes can interact with themselves to produce what are called "self-loops" and that these edges (while undirected and all of the same strength here) can also convey this more sophisticated information in the general case. A summary statistic about edges that is often looked at in network science is the connectance which is just the proportion of all edges possible that are actually present in a given network. Here, that value is about $0.5$

next slide

One step up in complexity of network structure is to look at properties of individual nodes in the network, for instance their degree which is just another way to say the number of links connected to that node.

Network structure: Global

Nestedness

Modularity

Bipartite

Weitz, et al. 2013
Wright & Reeves 1992
Ulrich, Almeida-Neto, & Gotelli 2008
Newman 2006

Especially in ecology, two "global" network structures have dominated investigations into network structure. These structures involve every node in the network, and are usually summarised with a single value.

The first of these popular ecological structures is "Nestedness" which is a attempt to quantify the degree to which the interactions of specialists are a subset of generalists' interactions. This results in a triangular network representation as seen in this modified figure of phages infecting bacteria from Joshua Weitz's group at Georgia Tech.

next slide

The second structure is called "Modularity," also called compartmentalization. This structure asks to what degree can species be divided into subgroups which interact strongly within the group, but weakly (or not at all in this toy example) with members of other such groups.

For each of these structures, multiple methods of quantification have been proposed, but they are, in-general, computationally intensive to do well

next slide

As a side note, these networks have an additional structure that comes up throughout this talk: they are bipartite. This means that, unline the toy network in the previous slide, in which any node can be connected to any other node, in these networks, the nodes can be divided into two classes for which all interactions are between nodes of disparate class. In this example, all links connect a virus to a bacterium; there are no links betweeen two viruses or between two bacteria.

Network structure: Local

Milo et al. 2002
Borrelli 2015

While the examination of the properties of small webs in isolation dates back decades, more recently these subgraphs or "motifs" have been interpreted as "building blocks" of larger networks.

For example, any network can be broken down into each possible combination of three connected nodes in the network, of which there are then only 13 possible configurations for the links to take. We can then tally up how many of each of these configurations there are in a given network or set of networks.

next slide

For example, in this study by Jonathan Borrelli, now at SUNY Plattsburgh, we see that across 50 food-webs we find that the same subgraphs are more or less common in each of the analyzed webs.

This work has been something of a perpetual work in progress over the past five years, so I am now in the process of writing it up as a challenge to the scientific community to see if they might be able to make some more progress on it

The Question:

Is it possible to distinguish networks of differing types by their structure?

Interaction Type:
the real-world-event being depicted by a network link
Network Structure:
the arrangement of connections within a network

The project arose from a fairly simple question, with some surprisingly complicated secondary considerations. First, we should probably clear up some of the terminology.

next slide

By types, we are mainly thinking about the type of ecological interaction being represented, e.g. pollination, parasitism, seed-dispersal, etc..

next slide

and by structure, we mean all of those arrangements of links mentioned in the introduction and any others that we can think of. Here we are only restricted by the computational effort required to measure something on many networks, some of which can be quite large.

Also, just to be clear, in this project we were only looking at bipartite networks, as this tends to be the structure of type-specific ecological interaction networks.

The Solution:

Seeking generality, specificity, and scalability.

Generality:
Will our model work on new data?
Specificity:
Will our model fail when it is supposed to?
Scalability:
What is the proper level of disambiguation?

It is important to be up front about what we are looking for as a solution to this problem.

Generality

In this and the following slides, I will be demonstrating a potential solution which has some success, but several sortcomings as well. For these figures, I am using a PCA that has been fit using a subset of non-ecological networks including webs of actors performing in the same movies, authors publishing in the same journals, crimes committed in different neighborhoods in different cities, legislature voting records, and mirobiome OTU presence/absence data across sites on human hosts.

These non-ecological data have an advantage of being fairly large and consistent in their collection methods, as well as being very highly replicated compared to ecological data.

One of the best ways to demonstrate that you are not overfitting your model. That is, that you are not fitting your model to a particular set of data rather than to the general class of data you are interested in. Is to divide your data into fitting and testing sets. Here, we use $100$ networks from each type to fit the PCA and then ...

next slide

we can add the leftover networks onto the same plot and see if they still fall within the same general areas.

Specificity

The second concern is a tradeoff with generality. We want our model to be able to be broken when we know that the relevant structure is disrupted. Put another way, a model that is able to fit any set of networks will ...

To test this, we randomize the structure of each of our networks according to a randomization protocol and see whether or not this breaks the clustering. We do this randomization in two ways, first a randomization that preserved degree distribution: each node in the randomized web has the same number of connections as in the original network, but who those connections are with is different

next slide

Second, we did a more conservative randomization in which only the total number of connections in the network is preserved.

next slide

As you can see, in this latter case, there is a significant loss of separation, especially with regards to the authorship networks and (to a lesser degree) the legislature webs.

Scalability

Finally, we have thus far treated the difference between type of network loosely. This was on purpose, but there isn't an obvious cutoff for type ...

next slide

For example, whe we look more closely at the crime networks, there is additional structure being captured by the PCA: namely each city's webs cluster together within the larger cluster of crime webs.

Efforts thus far

So, how do ecological networks fit into this attempt?

Unfortunately, despite relative success in each of the desired properties of a solution laid out thus far, ecological networks do not cluster.

One of the great things about this project is that everyone has their own ideas for which structures might be important. With an aim to utilize this crowd wisdom, we will be putting the whole dataset as well as well-anotated and easy-to-modify code online so everyone can give it a shot themselves.

Future directions

  • Machine learning
    • Gradient Boosted Regression Trees
  • Continuing to add data
    • Ecological data as available
    • Enhancing/adding to non-ecological data

But why didn't our method work? Maybe we didn't measure the right metrics or maybe PCA is too simplistic of a clustering technique. Alternatively, maybe the ecological networks we have are of too-low quality to be able to get at the fine-scale structural differences between networks of mutualistic and antagonistic interactions.

An alternative interpretation is that it actually did work. Maybe it is just that the variation within ecological networks is greater than the variation between networks of differing interaction types.

To address this latter point, I will be continuing to add data to the repository, but I am also interested in trying out some of the machine learning techniques which are a bit more sophisticated than PCA and have been growing in reputation in recent years.

Ecological Networks

Camerano 1880

The complication isn't difficult to appreciate: in 1880, an Italian Ecologist drew out the earliest food-web we have record of today. In this depiction, we see a web where species are arranged by trophic level and is little more than a combination a few food-chains. He also drew this web which now includes parasites -- the increase in complexity is self-evident.

introduction

What are parasites?

  • Prey/predator body size ratio (?)
  • Intimate, durable interactions (?)
  • Number of hosts or prey attacked during a particular life-history stage

Lafferty 2008

Lafferty, in his contribution to the Encyclopedia of Ecology, explains some of the many complications that arise when trying to systematically differentiate parasites from other life-cycles we observe in nature He discounts the first option since many predators consume prey that are larger than themselves (e.g. wolves eating moose) and the second with a similar argument: lions spend weeks consuming a single antelope.

Though he settles on the number of hosts/prey attacked in a particular life-history stage as a good indicator, with parasites attacking fewer (usually only one), this is difficult to measure quickly and precisely using the sampling methods that are common when collecting food-web data.

introduction: Food-Webs

This is a toy example of a food-web. Note that the links point from prey to predator. This allows us to distinguish between in-degree -- the number of prey a species has -- and out-degree -- the number of predators.

When viewed this way, we can use additional information to know that the owl is a predator and the tick a parasite, but theoretical ecologists tend to see this picture a little differently...

next slide

When the additional information of ecological classifications is unavailable, e.g. with a previously unobserved species, parasites and consumers look much more similar.

This project started as a question of whether or not we can identify species roles based just on this information, that is, the network structure.

Put another way, can we do what an ecologist does when observing species extensively in the field by just looking at the binary network of interactions?

By approaching this problem in a more statistical way, we can more explicitly identify the important, distinguishing characteristics, and provide justification (or not) for the characteristics that are thought to be important.

Every network can also be viewed as a matrix. When we do this, the row and column labels are in the same order, and a 1 indicates that the column species is eating the row species.

One feature of food-webs viewed in this way is that they tend to have a "cascade structure" -- that is, the interactions tend to show a pattern in which the majority of the links fall above the diagonal (i.e. in the upper triangle).

next slide

One unique feature of symbionts, such as parasites, is that they can be consumed by "accident" when their host is consumed. Note that these links tend to fall in the lower triangle. This difference is one of the structural cues we can use to distinguish species from one another.

methods: Group Model

  • Also known as the stochastic blockmodel
  • A generalized form of Modularity
  • Seeks to find groups of strongly connecting species

Allesina & Pascual 2009

Ecological classifications, such as herbivore or parasite, are assigned by ecologists following a detailed investigation of the species life history and, in particular, its interactions with other species. Another approach to group species is to look at the overall network structure and to group species that eat or are eaten by the same or similar prey or predators.

In 2009, Allesina & Pascual introduced the group model to ecology to make this concept statistically rigorous. The model had been used previously in the social sciences under a different name -- the stochastic blockmodel. This model works by re-arranging the rows/columns of the matrix in order to form blocks of species with similar interactions.

Summary

  • Ecological classifications have statistical meaning
    • Especially for Consumers and Parasites
    • (beyond simple degree distribution)
  • But this is not always the strongest source of variance
    • Further structure to be found
  • Concomitant links important
  • Next step: Identify the most explanatory properties of homogenous groups

So what can we take away from this?

Well, it is comforting to know that the classifications that ecologists use in the field have statistical meaning with regards to network structure, and that these differences extend beyond simple degree distribution (the degree-corrected model produced more imbalanced partitions, suggesting that discounting the effect of degree in fact improved the ability to distinguish ecological categorizations).

Interestingly, the intra-category differences were stronger than the inter-category differences in some cases, suggesting there might be more informative classifications than the one we used here.

Concomitant links are useful, but not the end-all in distinguishing parasites and consumers.

Finally, we can use these groupings (especially the homogenous ones) to identify the properties that differ between categorizations. These differences can then be used to produce more rigorous definitions and provide insight into the important distinctions between species roles in nature.

In particular, we revisit a traditional dogma of the stability implications for the relation between intraspecific competion (competition between members of the same species) and interspecific competition (competition between members of different species).

Dogma

Intraspecific competition must be greater than interspecific competition for stable coexistence

$\frac{\mathrm{d} N_1}{\mathrm{d} t}$$=$ $N_1$$($$r_1$$-$ $a_{12}$$N_2$$-$$a_{11}$ $N_1$$)$

$\frac{\mathrm{d} N_2}{\mathrm{d} t}$$=$ $N_2$$($$r_2$$-$ $a_{21}$$N_1$$-$$a_{22}$ $N_2$$)$

Condition for coexistence:

$a_{12}a_{21}$$<$$a_{11}a_{22}$

Chesson 2000

In almost every textbook on basic ecology, you will find a statement similar to this, stemming from the formulation of compeition using the Lotka-Volterra competition equations.

next slide

which can be written out in this way where the $r$'s are intrinsic growth rates, the $N$'s are population levels, and the $a$'s indicate the strength of interaction either with members of different species ($a_{ij}$, $i \neq j$) or the same species ($a_{ii}$)

next slide

The stability conditions for two species interacting in this way can be depicted in phase diagrams such as these (in which I've fixed the intra-specific competition and growth rates to be $1$ for convenience). For this system, there are four possible cases. In the top right species $1$ outcomptes species $2$ and drives the former extinct. In the bottom left is the complement of that scenario

In th bottom right, the winner is determined by the starting conditions, and the only situation in which both species coexist is the top right where for both species, the strength of competition with the other species is less than one.

More formally,

next slide

Summary

  • Two species coexistence: simple and intuitive
  • But this rule does not hold for more species
  • Patterns to exploit (for symmetric case):
    • Clustering most stable
    • Nested least stable
    • Intraspecific competition follows same patterns as inter-
  • Asymmetric competition much more difficult to study

So, in summary, while the two species rule is nice it is unfortunately not very useful as a maxim for real world competition networks. Nevertheless, there are some general patterns that emerge when we examine the extrema of stability.

The study of asymmetric competition would be a valuable next step, but is mathematically and computationally more complicated.

That's the last project I'm highlighting today. I just want to close with a little bit of synthesis and potential expansions in the area of ecological network structure.

Synthesis

  • Ecological networks are diverse and complex
  • But there are detectable patterns with real, ecological implications
    • Ecological interaction networks have large within-type variation and are more structurally similar than previously appreciated
    • Trophic roles have structural signatures in food-webs
    • The extreme cases of competition network structure have clear ecological corollaries

While it is true that at first-glance these three projects appear to have little in common, each of them looks at the relationship between network structure and ecological processes underlying the construction and maintenance of those networks.

In particular, I want to highlight that there are patterns in ecological network structure that exist to be found and explored further. While the first project's main message was that such structures can be difficult to find, even in this "negative" result is information on the variance inherent to ecological systems. in the second project, we find that there are structural signatures for the commonly described trophic roles, even parasites which are notoriously difficult to define precisely. Finally, in this last section, I've shown the endpoints in a spectrum of stability for competetive networks, revealing that both ends have clear, interpretable structures.

Extension

Pilosof, et al. 2017

I want to end with a note on two areas of growing research that I think will be fruitful avenues for Ecology in the near future: multi-layer networks and dynamic networks. Both of these are extensions to more tradtional network representations, incorporating greater realism at the expense of greater complexity. It will be important in the coming years to both develop methods for understanding these new data representations, but also to critically evaluate whether this complexity is worth it in terms of greater understanding or predictability.
