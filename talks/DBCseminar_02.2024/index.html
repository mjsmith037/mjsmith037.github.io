The relationship between the structure of these networks and the dynamics of the processes these neworks represent has been the central focus of my research for the past decade.

How does the structure of a network relate to the dynamical processes being depicted?

How does the structure of endophytic resource competition relate to the growth and health of host plants?

How does the positioning of parasites within food-webs relate to groupings of species based on interaction similarity?

How does the structure of bipartite ecological interaction networks differ between antagonism and mutualism?

How does the structure of competition networks relate to communities' dynamical stability?

How does the structure of metapopulations of host movement relate to local disease dynamics?

How does the structure of contact networks relate to the spread of disease?

As alluded previously, the key feature of this model for our purposes is that, depending on the relationship between just two parameters, the model could exhibit a range of possible disease dynamics, which we discretized into:

next slide

The pathogen going extinct, the disease prevalence approaching a steady level through time, and the disease prevalence exhibiting some oscillatory behavior through time (i.e. going through periods of high and low prevalence)

Thus, the fundamental question of this project can be summed up as: given two farms with known disease dynamics in isolation,

next slide

how do these dynamics change when we introduce host movement from one to the other?

next slide

and does this effect depend on the dynamics at the source of the movement?

In general, the specifics of these timelines do not matter as much as the overall regime (oscillatory, steady-state, etc.) being shown. In the previous slide, we considered when identical farms were connected, but what if the connected farms differed in their dynamical regimes

next slide

for instance, one having cyclical dynamics as before, but the other exhibiting steady state dynamics. What happens when we introduce a flow of individuals between these two populations?

next slide

When the flow of individuals is from the cycles to the steady, we see a somewhat expected result: we introduce cycles to the blue farm. I say expected, because when prevalence is high in the orange farm, a higher proportion of hosts moving to the blue farm will be infectious, while it will be more susceptible and removed individuals when prevalence is low. Such variability in movement might likewise introduce variability into the destination. The more interesting result is that when the flow is reversed

next slide

we see not only a reduction in cycles, but a qualitative change in dynamics, making the orange farm exhibit steady-state prevalence as well. In the supplementary slide below, we show that, while the rate of movement required to elicit each of these changes differs, very small rates of migration are needed to get this qualitative change.

Observing that the dynamical change depends on the regime of the origin patch leads to an obvious follow up question

So, we have three results for our small, toy networks, but how do these results apply in the context of larger, more realistic networks?

next slide

If migration in general reduces cycle amplitudes, we might expect networks with longer chains to have less variance in prevalence

next slide

Since destination patches tend to inherit the dynamics of origin patches, interconnected parts of the network should have similar dynamics.

next slide

But since there is an asymmetry in this inheritance, we expect there to be more cyclical dynamics than steady-states in general

To test these predictions, in the absence of good-quality, large empirical datasets, we generated a suite of random networks according to five distinct random-graph generating algorithms, an representative network of each is shown here. For each 100 random graphs in each of these general types, we randomized local parameters in each patch and then ran simulated epidemics and evaluated the qualitative dynamical regime of each patch after a sufficiently long time for transient dynamics to have passed.

next slide

These are filled bar charts, where each patch in the network is a horizontal line, and vertical lines represent unique simulation runs. if the bar is completely one color (e.g. blue) from top to bottom, then every patch in the metapopulation had the same dynamics at the end of the simulation (e.g. cycles). If, instead, the color varies from top to bottom, then the dynamics vary across the network.

In accordance with our third prediction, we see mostly cyclical dynamics, but there was also an interesting difference between the left three network types and the right two. While the left networks tended to have either all cyclical or all steady state dynamics. On the right, we see much more heterogeneity across the metapopulation. Further analysis suggests that the key driver of this effect is variance in in-degree (i.e. some patches receive migrants from few other patches, while others receive from many).

  • Simple patterns emerge in small metapopulation networks
    • When linked with migration, cyclical dynamics are dampened further down chains
    • Migration tends to lead to inheritance of dynamical regimes between populations
    • When multiple source populations are considered, more complicated dynamics (cycles and chaos) dominate the inheritance
  • While there are patterns in larger networks, they are not simple
    • Patches with no immigration tend to have higher pathogen variance than patches that have at least one source of incoming host movement
    • Most patches exhibit oscillatory dynamics
  • Further research is needed to:
    • Identify relationships between more complex network structures and disease dynamics
    • Fit to empirical data, including empirical network structures

In summary, we found relatively simple, clear patterns when we consider the case of just a few patches connected in simple ways, as noted previously.

next slide

In contrast, the results for larger networks were more complicated. While they were in agreement with some of our predictions from small networks, it is much more difficult to predict the dynamical regime of patches because of the complex, transitive relationships in larger, more realistc networks.

next slide

Further research is really needed to dig into these complexities and identify relationships between measures of network structure and the dynamical regimes we observe. Of course, the collection of high-quality empirical data is also essential to assess the applicability of the theory outlined here.

This project arose out of an observation in the early days of the COVID-19 pandemic, where the public health messaging of hand-washing, masking, and social distancing all seemed to be focused on changing individual behavior,

but we know that people do not exist in isolation, and the complex networks of social interactions we all take part in have important effects on the spread of disease through a community.

To do this we had to answer a couple of basic questions, such as "What is a contact?" The answer to this question is obviously context specific, but with COVID-19, with its aerosolized transmission, we think about the risk of transmission increasing with the duration of people sharing the same air space.

next slide

But of course the ways in which we are close to other people is also context dependent, leading to the key insight for this project: the structure of our interactions with other people is dependent on the different ways in which we interact with different facets of our social network.

To put it more concretely, interactions are structured differently among co-workers than among people at home, or at a religious institution. The differences in these interactions, including the strength of interaction and the interacting group size, can have consequences for the spread of disease.

Disease Model

I want to take a minute to run through the underlying disease model, but will keep this brief as it is not the most important aspect of this project.

At each timestep in a simulation, every individual in the network has one of six states. They start out as Susceptible to disease and can become infected when they share an edge, representing a social contact, with an infectious individual.

next slide

Upon infection, they enter a latency period and are classified as Exposed, but not yet infectious. Over time, they transition to being I/Infectious, and some fraction will develop symptoms.

next slide

As more time passes, some of these will Recover,

next slide

while others will sadly Die.

next slide

The final piece of the puzzle is that those individuals that we classified as vulnerable have both longer disease courses (slower recovery),

next slide

and are more likely to die, modifying these two parameters as shown.

What does this look like in a single simulation?

So, for a single simulated epidemic, we see nodes cycling through the various disease states, and, on the right, the proportion of the population in each class through time. Note that some individuals are able to escape infection, even at the end of the simulation, if they are sparsely connected to the rest of the network, or just very lucky.

We can summarize the outcome of a single epidemic simulation in different ways, such as the epidemic peak (the maximum proportion of the population ever concurrently infectious), the percentage of vulnerable individuals in the population that get sick at some point during the simulation, or the total percent of the population that dies from the disease.

For these first results, we compare the two age/household-size distributions, a "Florida-like" and a "Texas-like" population. The latter sees a higher epidemic peak and percentage of vulnerable individuals infected. This is likely driven by the larger household sizes, driving faster and wider pathogen spread.

Counter-intuitively, however, significantly more people die in the Florida-like population. This is because, even though the epidemics are smaller, there is a much higher proportion of vulnerable individuals in the Florida-like population, leading to higher mortality rates.

We can also consider the relative contribution of each of our interaction network layers, here focusing on the contrast of classmate and co-worker interactions. We focus on this comparison because, at the time of writing, there was a large discussion about the relative merits of opening schools or workplaces as a phasing out of widespread lockdowns.

Our results showed that workplaces were more consequential (higher absolute value of effect size) for all measures of epidemic severity, including epidemic peak, total number of individuals infected, and rate of epidemic spread.

This is certainly influenced by our assumptions of network construction: we have more co-worker links in our networks than classmate. However, this decision of network construction is informed by the real-world: there are more working-age individuals than school-age children in the populations we consider and workplaces can be many times larger than schools and classrooms, so we do not view this as a limiation of the analysis per se.

Finally, we considered the effects of differential risk-tolerance through the population. For all previous results, we considered a case where all individuals participated in all of their respective interactions. Here we show the effect of different rates of transmission between classmates or coworkers on the epidemic peak and the total number of vulnerable individuals infected. As expected, higher rates of transmission result in more disease (top right of each heatmap).

next slide

We next consider a situation where vulnerable individuals are less risk-tolerant, that is, they take steps to reduce their infection risk through avoiding school or workplace interactions. In this case, we see a moderate reduction in disease, but mostly at low transmission rates.

next slide

We then asked, what if not only the vulnerable individuals themselves, but all members of their households likewise isolated. This would be like you deciding to work remotely because you live with your elderly parent/grandparent. In this case, we see a dramatic reduction in disease and a near-complete elimination of disease among vulnerable individuals.

Astute viewers might at this point be thinking "of course there is less disease, you've just removed a ton of links from the network!"

next slide

Yet, it is not the removal of links per-se, but rather the selective fragmentation of the contact network that yields such dramatic effects. When we remove the same number of links randomly (i.e. irrespective of relation to vulnerable individuals), we re-capture the no-difference levels of disease.

Conclusions

  • Populations are highly interconnected, allowing disease to spread even to specific individuals that are taking precautions
  • Community action is needed to protect the most vulnerable
  • Demographic structure can have nuanced, contradictory effects on disease spread
  • Framework as an educational tool

These results highlight how modern societies are highly interconnected, and how these connections make public health an intrinsically community-level problem. Individuals are not able to protect themselves, they need community action to be effective.

We also highlight how demography can have complex, sometimes counter-intuitive effects on disease spread.

Finally, we have taken this modelling framework and built an educational tool to help understand the relationship between network structure and disease spread
