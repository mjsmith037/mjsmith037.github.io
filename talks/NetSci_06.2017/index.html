JEROME VALLETTE/AFP/Getty Images

When we see images of rainforests such as this, there are two striking observations that can be made. First, every square meter of land appears to be covered by a tree, each of which is approximately the same height and size. The second is that, despite this apparent homogeneity, a hectare of rainforest can support hundreds of unique species, all coexisting despite an onslaught of perturbing forces. As theoretical ecologists, we would like to develop a theory that can explain this, allowing for the stable coexistence of many species while maintaining a robustness to parameters.

Nacho Doce/Reuters

In many ways, solving this problem comes down to figuring out what happens when an individual dies or, put another way, how the replacement is chosen to fill the gap.

Model Assumptions

$$\sum_i x_i = 1$$

A key assumption of the model we will build is inspired by that first observation of the rainforest: that the space is entirely filled and any gap in the canopy is rapidly filled by a new individual. Because of this, the dynamics can be said to be appproximately "zero-sum."
This means that the number of individuals in the community is approximately fixed and we can just model the relative species abundances $x_i$. Pictorally, this means that:

Building a Model

$$\propto f_i x_i$$

$$\begin{align}\mathcal{P}(i \text{ wins against } j) &= H_{ij}\\ &= 1 - H_{ji}\end{align}$$

$$d_i x_i$$

We can model the processes at play in a more mathematically rigorous manner. For instance,
next slide
each species has a birth rate, which is proportional to its fecundity (the number of seedlings produced).
next slide
It also has a death rate. We assume that a random individual dies at each time point. Since the individual is chosen at random, the probability of any individual dying is proportional to its relative species abundance. Finally, some things happen in-between these events, which we will model as competition.
next slide
We can assume very simplified competition, where the individuals compete in pairs and the winner of any competitive bout is determined by a tournament matrix $\textbf{H}$ which encapsulates the probabilities of any species $i$ beating another species $j$. Of course, this means species $j$ has a $1-H_{ij}=H_{ji}$ probability of winning the bout.

Relation to Prior Models

  • Neutral Theory of Biodiversity (Hubbell 2001)
  • $$f_i = d_i = 1 \text{, } H_{ij} = H_{ji} = 0.5$$
  • Competitive Tournaments (Allesina & Levine 2011)
  • $$f_i = d_i = 1 \text{, } (H_{ij}, H_{ji}) = \begin{cases} (1,0)&\\ (0,1) \end{cases}$$
  • Competitive Hypertournaments
  • $$f_i = d_i = 1 \text{, } \text{any nonnegative } (H_{ij}, H_{ji}): H_{ij} + H_{ji} = 1$$
  • General Model
  • $$\text{any nonnegative } f_i, d_i, (H_{ij}, H_{ji}): H_{ij} + H_{ji} = 1$$

Using this simple framework, we can recapitulate some models that have already been well-studied, as well as propose some advancements. For instance, the neutral theory of biodiversity proposed by Stephen Hubbell in 2001 can be replicated in this framework by assuming
next slide
that the fecundities and death rates are equal for all species (we can consequently assume that this value is $1$ for conveneince) and that the probability of any given species winning a competitive bout is equal to that of its competitor winning.
next slide
Likewise, if the probabilities of victory are exclusively $1$'s and $0$'s, that is, species $i$ always wins against species $j$ and always loses to some species $k$, we are dealing with competitive tournaments, which have been explored in an ecological context by Allesina and Levine in PNAS in 2011.
In this work, we build on these models by first allowing non-binary probabilities of victory
next slide
and second allowing full flexibility of parameterization, breaking that reliance on equal physiological parameters.
next slide
We will first consider the former and get to the latter toward the end of the presentation.

Building a Model

$$\propto f_i x_i$$

$$\begin{align}\mathcal{P}(i \text{ wins against } j) &= H_{ij}\\ &= 1 - H_{ji}\end{align}$$

$$d_i x_i$$

Assume $f_i = d_i = 1 \quad \forall i$

$\dfrac{\text{d} x_i }{\text{d} t} = $ $x^2_i$ $+$ $2 x_i \displaystyle\sum_{j \neq i} H_{ij} x_j$ $-$ $x_i$

Looking at our three processes again, this means by assuming constant and equivalent fecundities and death rates, we can
next slide
codify this into equations where the birth and death rates are proportional to the relative abundance of the species and growth in this abundance can be modified by the outcome of interactions with other species. Note that this is multiplied by two since species are chosen as pairs, and the order of the pairs does not matter. That is, $i$ vs. $j$ is the same as $j$ vs $i$.

Building a Model: Equilvalence with Replicator Equation

$$\begin{align} \dfrac{\text{d} x_i }{\text{d} t} &= x^2_i + 2 x_i \sum_{j \neq i} H_{ij} x_j - x_i\\ &= x_i \left(2 \sum_j H_{ij} x_j - 1 \right)\\ &= \sum_j \left( H_{ij} - H_{ji}\right) x_i x_j\\ &= \sum_j P_{ij} x_i x_j \end{align}$$

One of the really nice things about this model formulation is that it can be shown to be equivalent to the extremely well studied replicator equations, using the notation from game theory of a payoff matrix $\mathbf{P}$ which dictates how much each species gains (or loses) as a result of each competitive bout. This equivalence allows us to leverage results and tools from this additional field of study.

Building a Model: Example

$$\mathbf{H} = \left[\begin{array} 00.5 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.5 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0.5 \end{array}\right] $$


$$\mathbf{P} = \left[\begin{array} 0~~~0 & ~~~1 & -1 \\ -1 & ~~~0 & ~~~1 \\ ~~~1 & -1 & ~~~0 \end{array}\right]$$

Lets look at a concrete example. Imagine three species: rock, paper, and scissors which interact in an intransitive cycle of complete dominance. The tournament matrix $\mathbf{H}$ is thus shown here, with a $1$ in the top middle, for instance, indicating that paper always wins against rock. An individual of a given species always has an equal probability of winning or losing when competing against another individual of the same species, hence the $0.5$ on the diagonal. This $\mathbf{H}$ corresponds to the following $\mathbf{P}$, which shows that, in the previously mentioned bout, the result is that rock loses an individual and paper gains one. These dynamics result is neutral cycling about an equilibrium of one third for each species.

Coexistence

How many species do we expect to survive?

Method

  1. Generate many random (hyper) tournaments of $s$ species by drawing interaction strengths($H_{ij}$) at random
  2. Run dynamics
  3. Count the $k$ survivors
  4. what is $\mathcal{P}(k|s)$?

$$\mathcal{P}(k|s) = \begin{cases} 0 &\text{for } k \text{ even}\\ {{s}\choose{k}} 2^{1-s} &\text{otherwise}\end{cases}$$

Result

So, we saw that in some cases, all of the species will survive and in other cases, some will go extinct. It is a reasonable step to then wonder how many species will survive on average. Put another way, if we were to open all the gates at a zoo and let the animals interact, how many species would we expect to coexist after all of the extinctions? We can approach this problem from the perspective of random matrices. In this approach, we generate a random tournament matrix of probabilities and then run the dynamics to see how many of the original species survive. If we do this many times, we can then calculate the expected number.

next slide
When we do this, we find that this probability is $0$ for an even number of surviving species (as was also shown by Allesina & Levine 2011) and follows a binomial distribution with $p = 0.5$ otherwise. This means that, regardless of the number of initial species, the expected number that survive is half of that. Put another way, there is no upper limit for the number of surviving species, which suggests that the high biodiversity we observe in nature maybe shoudldn't be so surprising after all.

Dynamics:
Neutral Cycles




$\ast$

One of the interesting things about the transition from strict tournaments to hyper tournaments, i.e. moving from binary probabilities of victory to real-valued probabilities, is that we can actually construct an infitinte number of matrices $\mathbf{H}$ that will converge to an arbitrary equilibrium $x^\ast$. What this means is that we can fine-tune a system to allow the persistence of an even number of species.
next slide
Yet, these cases are highly degenerate and will collapse into an odd number of species with any modification of the coeficients of $\mathbf{H}$ or parameters $f_i$ or $d_i$.

Conclusions

For any matrix $\mathbf{H}$ on $s$ species

  • Irrespective of (positive) initial conditions, $k$ species will coexist and $s - k$ will go extinct
  • Those that coexist, cycle neutrally around the equilibrium point $x^\ast$
  • We can choose $x^\ast$ arbitrarily

For a random $\mathbf{H}$ on $s$ species

  • On average, $k = s/2$
So, where do we stand thus far? We have a model that can produce high biodiversity without fine tuning. It can, though I did not go into it here, yield an arbitrary species abundance distribution as equilibrium with the proper choice of $\mathbf{H}$. There are still some shortcomings, namely, these neutral cycles, which are not only biologically unrealistic, but also make their species more susceptible to extinction as perturbations, on average, increase the size of such neutral cycles.
next slide

Higher-order Interactions

At this point, you might be wondering what this has to do with higher-order interactions (HOI). So far, the interaction part of our model consisted of choosing two seedlings and competing them to determine the winner in a pairwise fasion. This is overwhelmingly the approach used in cological research, but in nature, we observe a rich seedbank, where potentially many seedlings are competing for each gap in the canopy.
next slide
We can take a step toward this reality by increasing the number of competing species in each bout to three.
next slide

Parameterizing HOI

Unfortunately, even this small change faces us with a dilemma. We can consider true HOI in which there is a coeficient for each unique triplet of interacting species, but then our number of parameters in the model quickly becomes intractable from an empirical standpoint. An alternative is to consider an interaction chain,
next slide
in which first two of the species compete and then the winner of this pairwise bout competes with the third species to determine the overall winner. This has the advantage of reducing the number of parameters, but comes with its own shortcomming: now the order of interactions matters. For instance, in this example, if we re-ordered the interactions such that the orange seedling interacts first with the purple seedling, rather than the blue, we see that it is purple, rather than orange that advances to the second bout and if blue is able to defeat purple then the final victor can also differ.
next slide

Still Consistent with Replicator Dynamics

$$\dfrac{\text{d} x_i }{\text{d} t} = \sum_j P_{ij} x_i x_j$$ $$\dfrac{\text{d} x_i }{\text{d} t} = \sum_j P_{ijk} x_i x_j x_k$$

where $\mathbf{P}$ is a tensor in which $$P_{ijk} = 2 H_{ij} H_{ik} - H_{ji} H_{jk} - H_{ki} H_{kj}$$

This change does not, however, break equivalence with replicator dynamics. Instead, the payoff matrix now becomes
next slide
a payoff tensor with as many indices as there are interacting seedlings in each competetive bout. Furthermore, because we are defining the HOI in terms of a sequence of pairwise competitions, we can write this tensor $\mathbf{P}$ in terms of the coefficients of the tournament matrix $\mathbf{H}$.

Conclusions

  • The addition of higher order interactions stabilize the dynamics but do not change the equilibrium
  • Higher orders of higher order interactions speed up convergence to the equilibrium
  • This is all despite not adding any new information/parameters into the model!
This small, and rather intuitive, modification to our dynamical model has a profound effect on the dynamics, changing from neutral cycles to convergence to a fixed point. This convergence can be speeded by increasing the number of individuals competing in each bout. Finally, and most surprisingly, this is all without adding any information into the system -- we still use the pairwise interaction probabilities to determine the victor of these higher-order interactions.

Conclusions

  • This theory relies on higher-order interactions
  • Leads to the stable coexistence of an arbitrary number of species
    • Can produce any species abundance distribution
  • Is robust to changes in parameters
  • More experimentally tractable due to parameterization of HOI as direct consequence of pairwise interactions
    • Such interactions chains can arise in nature due to differing scales of space or time
In conclusion, we have presented a theory which allows for the coexistence of very many species, even in the case of randomized parameters. With the addition of higher-order interactions, the dynamics are stabilized, and this stability is enhanced by incorporating "higher" higher-order interactions. Remarkably, this formulation can yield dynamics leading to an arbitrarily specified equilibrium, which is valuable insofar as it means it can reproduce observed distributions of relative abundances.
Our use of interaction chains of pairwise competitions rather than "true" higher order interactions severely reduces the number of parameters in the model and makes it empirically testable.
Finally, if you see such interaction chains as a limiting assumption, this is actually a very natural assumption when considering differing scales of space or time. For instance, two seedlings could sprout more closely and only interact with a third after the first competition is already decided. Alternatively, there could be a temporal distinction in which the links of the interaction chain are separated by timescale.

Introducing Stochasticity

What happens when we add stochasticity to this framework? This plot depicts stochastic trajectories for a rock-paper-scissors system, when we vary the number of seedlings competing ($m$). Dotted lines correspond to the deterministic approximation. For $m = 1$ and equal physiological rates, one recovers a neutral model. For equal physiological rates and pairwise interactions ($m = 2$) stability is only neutral and the deterministic solution does not describe the stochastic trajectories. Densities fluctuate around the fixed point, but the amplitude of these oscillations is driven by stochasticity, rather than being fixed as in the deterministic case. For higher- order interactions ($m = 3$), densities fluctuate around the stable equilibrium of the deterministic system. When physiological rates are different, the stochastic trajectories are predicted by the deterministic equations. For $m \le 2$, extinctions occur very rapidly, and are driven by the deterministic dynamics. For $m = 3$, trajectories fluctuate around the stable equilibrium. For these simulations the total population abundance was set to $1000$.

Transitivity

It is a classic result for tournaments (binary competition probabilities) that intransitivity is necessary for coexistence. In contrast, when we allow for partial dominance in the form of hyper-tournaments we see that this intransitivity need only exist "in-probability" (i.e. rock need only beat scissors most of the time, etc.). Finally, when we move to the fully general model with variable physiological rates as well, we can even break this constraint. As seen in this example, we can have a matrix $\mathbf{H}$ which is completely transitive and yet produces stable coexistence.
