One step up in complexity of network structure is to consider the relationship between incoming and outgoing links in the same node. Here we plot the in-degree against the outdegree for each node in our netwokrks, dividing nodes according to the node's kingdom and the treatment of the host plant.

Note that all of these lines have negative slopes -- nodes with high indegrees tend to have low outdegree and vice versa. Put another way, there are some isolates which we think of as strong competitors: they overlap many other isolates' niches, but do not often have their own niche significantly overlapped; and there are some isolates we could think of as weak competitors: overlapping few others, yet having many overlap their own niche.

While this framework of measuring the degree of niche overlap between each pair of isolates provides a high level of detail about the structure of iteractions across the community, many of the most popular metrics of network structure are defined only in the case of binary networks, that is networks where the links only indicate presence or absence of an interaction, and do not provide information on the strength of any given interaction.

To convert our weighted interactions into binary ones, we need only define a threshold

One of the most commonly utilized network structures is that of centrality. There are many different formulations for centrality, but they all boil down to assigning importance to each node in the network. What they differ in is how they define "importance". Here, we report two measures of centrality that have some biological relevance to the system we are working with: degree and closeness.

Degree centrality is simply the number of connections each node is a part of, while closeness centrality is related to the distance between a given node and all other nodes in the network. In our case, degree centrality is how many isolates are directly competing with any given isolate, while closeness centrality is more a measure of indirect effects -- what is the average number of interaction steps between a given isolate and all of the other isolates in the network.

An isolate with high degree centrality has the potential to directly influence many of its neighbors, but an isolate with high closeness centrality has greater potential to spread its influence throughout the network as a whole.

Finally, we also report differences in the clustering coefficient of each node. This value is calculated by counting how many of an isolate's partners interact with each other, forming triangular patterns in the interaction network.

Each box in this table is a network, colored according to the sign and significance of differences between isolates of each of the two kingdoms: blue for bacteria, red for fungi. As can be seen, bacteria and fungi differ often in the control plants, but very rarely in the nutrient supplemented ones. This suggests some sort of equalizing force being applied in the NPK treatment.

Looking at global measures of network structure (those that take the whole network into account in their calculation), we can compare the treatments more directly. Here, we report the total clustering coefficient, the proportion of realized connections, intransitivity, some leading eigenvalues, counts of short loops, and finally some summary statistics from the degree distributions.

When we compare the 6 values for each of the control networks to the 6 values for NPK in each of these metrics (using t-tests), we see very few differences in structure between treatments. (Note that these results are qualitatively independent from the inclusion/exclusion of the outlying control network)

(Note that when the outlying control network is included in this analysis, a second significant difference is present in triad 2, where NPK plants have significantly higher counts than do control)
